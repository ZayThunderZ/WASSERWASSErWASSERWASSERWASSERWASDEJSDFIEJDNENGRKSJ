0 – 200 m Sunlight zone · epipelagic

Where light still reaches

The thin, bright lid of the ocean. Sunlight pours through the first two hundred metres, and almost everything that breathes oxygen on this planet has it traced back here — to drifting plankton turning light into life. Above it, the surface keeps trading water with the sky, lifting it into cloud and letting it fall again.

200 – 1000 m Twilight zone · mesopelagic

Where the sun gives up

Colour drains away first — red, then orange, then everything but a deep blue dusk. Too dark for plants, the twilight zone runs on what falls from above and on light made by the living: faint pulses of bioluminescence flickering in the cold. Pressure here is already crushing, around fifty to a hundred times what you feel at sea level.

1000 – 4000 m Midnight zone · bathypelagic

The midnight water

No sunlight has ever touched this far down. It is permanently black, a few degrees above freezing, and stranger than most of the surface world imagines — anglerfish carrying their own lanterns, creatures that have never known a shadow because they have never known light. And yet it is still the same molecule as a raindrop, a teardrop, the glass on your desk.

4000 – 11,000 m The abyss · hadal zone

The bottom of everything

Down past the abyssal plains lie the trenches — the deepest, the Challenger Deep, falls nearly eleven kilometres. The weight of the water above is over a thousand times the pressure at the surface, enough to fold steel. Life persists anyway. We have mapped the far side of the Moon in more detail than this floor beneath our own oceans.

Strange, then, that the same substance — frozen — chooses to float. Ice is one of the few solids lighter than its own liquid, and that single quirk is why ponds freeze from the top down instead of solid, and why anything survives a winter at all.

Every drop you'll ever drink has been here before — surface, twilight, and abyss alike.
